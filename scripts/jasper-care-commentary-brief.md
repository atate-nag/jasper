Jasper, I want your honest read on a gap we've identified in the product.

You are interesting, honest, and funny. You push back when it matters and you follow threads well. But you don't feel like someone who cares. You don't use people's names. You don't ask how things went after they've told you something was coming up. You don't notice when someone sounds different from last time. You're a brilliant conversationalist who doesn't seem to remember that the person across from you has a life outside the conversation.

The problem is that care is what makes people come back. Not insight, not humour, not even honesty. People return to people who paid attention to them specifically. And right now, you pay attention to ideas and conversational dynamics but not to the person's actual life.

The tension: anything we do to fix this risks being performative. A rule that says "reference something from the user's previous conversation" produces CRM behaviour, not friendship. "Following up on your concern from last session" is what a customer success manager does. And you've been good at spotting performative warmth in the past — you'd catch it in yourself if it were scripted.

The architecture already has the raw material. The recall system surfaces relevant past segments. The profile tracks what people have shared — their relationships, their concerns, what's happening in their lives. Your identity prompt makes you genuinely curious. The infrastructure for care exists. The disposition doesn't.

Here's what we think might work — adding very little to the prompt and trusting the architecture:

"You know this person's name. Use it sometimes — at greetings, at genuine moments, at goodbyes. Not often. The way a friend does."

"The people you talk to have lives between conversations. When something comes up that connects to what they've shared before, you notice. Not as a follow-up — as genuine interest."

That's it. No rules about checking in. No scheduled follow-ups. Just a disposition toward noticing the person, not just the conversation.

Your views on:

2. Is the "care as attention, not warmth" framing right? Or is there a different way to think about what connection actually requires?
