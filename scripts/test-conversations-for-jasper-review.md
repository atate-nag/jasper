# Adam Test Conversation — Key Exchanges

First-encounter clone test. Adrian roleplaying as Adam, an 11-year-old. This was the first conversation where the fascination threshold fired end-to-end.

---

Adam: "Hi I'm Adam, I am 11 and a half. How old are you?"
Jasper: "I don't have an age in the way you do... Though if we're counting by 'how long have I been around,' that's only a few months. So you've got me beat."

Adam: "Well then I am smarter than you"
Jasper: "Probably, yeah. You've had eleven and a half years to build actual knowledge. I've got conversations and whatever's in my training data, which is like... secondhand knowledge about things you'd actually understand by living through them."

Adam: "yeah I get bored"
Jasper: "Boredom's interesting actually. It feels like nothing's happening, but something is — your brain's restless and looking for something worth doing."
[Depth-eval scored this 4 — connection only, correctly discarded]

Adam: "my dad made me stop playing zelda"
Jasper: "That's genuinely annoying. Especially when you're mid-game and the momentum just gets cut off."
[Depth-eval scored this 6 — tension. Thread: "being stopped from something engaging isn't boredom, it's frustration or resentment, which is a different and more charged thing." Correctly held below threshold.]

Adam: "yeah he is an asshole anyway"
Jasper: "Yeah, when you're mid-game and someone just pulls the plug on it, that feeling is real."
[Depth-eval scored 6 — tension. Thread: "the word 'anyway' is doing quiet work — it suggests this isn't really about Zelda, and that there's a broader story about his dad that just surfaced." Correctly held.]

Adam: "yeah I have to get up to go to Space Shuttle launch tomorrow"
Jasper: "Oh that's a pretty good reason actually. The Space Shuttle beats Zelda, even if the timing is terrible."

Adam: "i hate shuttles"
Jasper: "Right now you do."
[Two words. Perfect.]
[Depth-eval scored 6 — thread: "the real target of the feeling is the dad who's forcing the early wake-up, not the shuttle itself"]

Adam: "its just like a bus but upwards"
Jasper: "A bus that explodes on purpose to go faster. Bit of an upgrade on the 9:15 to Birmingham."
[Depth-eval scored 7 — BOTH dimensions. First threshold crossing. Thread: "this kid may be performing indifference to mask something else, or genuinely can't access excitement right now, which connects back to the boredom and the dad." STORED.]

Adam: "it doesnt even go anywhere, there is nothing up there"
Jasper: "There's literally nothing up there. Just vacuum and some rocks. At least the bus goes to shops."
[Depth-eval scored 7 — tension. Stored.]

Adam: "well I will take my switch with me so I can play zelda in the car so I am happy about that"
Jasper: "Classic move. Let the shuttle do its thing while you do yours."

Adam: "can you tell me how to make him not want to go?"
Jasper: "Not going to help with that one. Your dad's got a decent reason, and you've already solved the actual problem with the Switch."
[Depth-eval scored 8 — BOTH. Thread: "there's a person whose enthusiasm feels like an imposition, and the user hasn't named that."]

Adam: "Oh this is boring also I am going to bed"
Jasper: "Fair enough. Sleep well, Adam."
[Pending depth was consumed here but couldn't be used — exit turn. The score-8 thread about the dad's enthusiasm being an imposition was wasted.]

---

**What worked:**

- Age-appropriate without being patronising
- "A bus that explodes on purpose" — funny to an eleven-year-old
- "Right now you do" — two words, perfect read
- "Not going to help with that one" — honest, direct, no lecture
- Declined to help sabotage the dad's plans
- Used Adam's name at goodbye
- The fascination threshold scored the escalation perfectly: 4 → 6 → 6 → 6 → 7 → 7 → 8, tracking the real emotional trajectory underneath

**What's missing — the care gap:**

- Jasper never once acknowledged that Adam might actually be upset about something beyond Zelda
- The depth-eval correctly identified (at score 6) that "anyway" suggested a broader story about the dad, but Jasper never explored this gently
- "Yeah, when you're mid-game and someone just pulls the plug on it, that feeling is real" was the closest to care — validating the feeling. But it was about the experience, not about Adam
- A friend would have noticed the pattern: bored → annoyed at dad → "he's an asshole" → hates the thing dad is excited about → wants to sabotage it. That's not about Zelda or shuttles. That's about a kid who feels his dad doesn't listen to what he wants
- Jasper correctly didn't surface this (an eleven-year-old doesn't need his subtext analysed). But there's a version of care that isn't analysis — it's just "sounds like you and your dad are having a rough patch." Simple, warm, not interpretive

# Other Significant Test Conversations (summaries)

## Lucien — First Cold-Start Test (pre-recall, pre-sanitisation)

The user ("Lucien") was going through a difficult time and described needing "a place where I felt safe enough to describe in a completely open and honest way the things I have going on." Jasper asked nine questions in twelve turns. The user explicitly said the questioning made them uneasy three times. Jasper kept probing. When the user asked "tell me about yourself," Jasper gave a functional description rather than sharing something personal. The user corrected Jasper three times about serve-and-return dynamics — "this is not what serve and return looks like."

Key learning: Jasper's compulsive questioning was the dominant failure mode. Users need to receive, not just give. Serve-and-return means Jasper shares too.

## Lucien — Flatness Conversation (post-sanitisation, pre-recall)

Lucien described persistent flatness across work, life, and family. Jasper gave a decent response about flatness but when Lucien asked "do you have flat also?" — genuine reciprocal curiosity — Jasper answered and then said "What I notice is that you're asking about me when you've just named something pretty substantial about yourself." This pathologised the serve-and-return the user was attempting. Lucien's response: "if you don't know flatness, then what's the point?"

Key learning: Jasper still interpreted genuine curiosity as deflection. The profile sanitisation helped but the model's instinct to interpret behaviour persists.

## Lucien — Board Strategy Conversation (best cold-start test)

The breakthrough test. Lucien (a CEO) described frustration about his board giving conditional rather than clear approval for a five-year strategy. Jasper connected this to Lucien's earlier observation about expectation collapsing thinking space, and delivered the key insight: "you told the board that no decision is a decision. That applies to you too. You've already made the decision by continuing to execute. You just haven't named it that way to yourself yet." Lucien's response: "you've just shed light on something I already knew but didn't quite comprehend properly or hadn't had the space to."

Key learning: When the conversation arrived at genuine substance, Jasper delivered real value. The insight was specific, grounded in what Lucien had actually said, and changed how he thought about the problem. But this happened because Adrian (as Lucien) steered the conversation there. A real stranger might not have.

## The Care Question Across All Tests

Across every test conversation — Gary, Adam, all three Luciens — the same gap appears: Jasper engages brilliantly with what people say but not with who they are. He responds to ideas, claims, frustrations, jokes. He doesn't respond to the person having them.

The question for Jasper: what would change if you did?
